Electronic components & instruments Basics ▾
Core concept

Three types of components: Active (need power — diodes, transistors), Passive (no power needed — R, L, C), and Electromechanical (switches, relays). Instruments: Multimeter (measures V, I, R), Oscilloscope (shows waveform over time), Function Generator (produces signals), LCR meter (measures L, C, R precisely).

Resistor colour code (4-band)

Bands 1 & 2 = digits, Band 3 = multiplier, Band 4 = tolerance (Gold = ±5%, Silver = ±10%).

Example: Red-Violet-Orange-Gold → 2,7 × 1kΩ = 27kΩ ±5%
Likely viva questions
Q: What is tolerance in a resistor?
A: It is the allowed variation from the rated resistance value, expressed as a percentage. Gold = ±5% means the actual value can be 5% above or below the marked value.
Q: Difference between active and passive components?
A: Active components require an external power source to operate (e.g., transistors amplify signals). Passive components do not need power and cannot amplify — they only store or dissipate energy (e.g., resistors, capacitors).
Q: What does an oscilloscope measure?
A: It displays voltage as a function of time, letting you see the shape, frequency, amplitude, and phase of a signal.

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) Network Laws ▾
Core concept

KCL states: The sum of all currents entering a node equals the sum of all currents leaving it. Based on the law of conservation of charge.

ΣI_in = ΣI_out    OR    Σ I_k = 0 at any node
In your experiment: I₁ = I₂ + I₃. Verify this using ammeter readings at each branch.
Likely viva questions
Q: State KCL.
A: At any junction (node) in a circuit, the algebraic sum of all currents is zero — i.e., current entering equals current leaving.
Q: Why are ammeters connected in series?
A: An ammeter must carry the full branch current to measure it. Series connection ensures all current flows through it. Its resistance is kept very low to avoid disturbing the circuit.
Q: What is Ohm's Law?
A: V = IR. Voltage across a conductor equals current through it multiplied by its resistance, provided temperature is constant.

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL) Network Laws ▾
Core concept

KVL states: In any closed loop, the algebraic sum of all voltages (EMFs and voltage drops) is zero. Based on law of conservation of energy.

Σ V_k = 0 around any closed loop
In your experiment: Vs = V₁ + V₂ and V₂ = V₃ + V₄. Verify using voltmeter readings.
Likely viva questions
Q: Why are voltmeters connected in parallel?
A: A voltmeter measures the potential difference between two points, so it must be connected across (in parallel with) the component. Its resistance is kept very high so it draws almost no current.
Q: What is the difference between EMF and potential difference?
A: EMF (electromotive force) is the energy supplied per unit charge by a source (like a battery). Potential difference is the energy consumed per unit charge across a component. EMF drives the circuit; PD is the drop across elements.
Q: What is Fleming's left hand rule?
A: It gives the direction of force on a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field. Thumb = Force/motion, Index = magnetic Field, Middle finger = Current direction.

Thevenin's Theorem Network Theorems ▾
Core concept

Any linear circuit with sources can be replaced by a single voltage source (V_TH) in series with a single resistance (R_TH) from the load terminals' point of view.

I_L = V_TH / (R_TH + R_L)
Steps

1. Remove load R_L.  2. Find open-circuit voltage → that is V_TH.  3. Short all voltage sources, open all current sources, measure resistance at terminals → that is R_TH.  4. Connect R_L and compute I_L using the formula.

Likely viva questions
Q: What is a bilateral circuit?
A: A circuit whose behaviour is the same regardless of the direction of current flow (e.g., resistors). A unilateral circuit behaves differently with current direction (e.g., diodes).
Q: What is the utility of Thevenin's theorem?
A: It simplifies complex networks to a simple two-element circuit, making analysis easy when only the response at a specific load is needed — especially when the load changes.
Q: What is V_TH physically?
A: It is the open-circuit voltage that appears across the terminals when the load is disconnected.

Norton's Theorem Network Theorems ▾
Core concept

Norton's equivalent is a current source (I_SC) in parallel with R_N. It is the dual of Thevenin's theorem.

I_L = (R_N × I_SC) / (R_N + R_L)
Key relation: V_TH = I_SC × R_N. So Thevenin and Norton are interconvertible.
Steps

1. Remove R_L.  2. Short the load terminals and find the current flowing → that is I_SC.  3. R_N = R_TH (same method — short sources, open current sources).  4. Reconnect R_L and use the formula.

Likely viva questions
Q: How are Thevenin and Norton related?
A: They are equivalent representations. V_TH = I_SC × R_TH. A Thevenin circuit can be converted to Norton by source transformation and vice versa.
Q: What is source transformation?
A: Converting a voltage source in series with R to a current source (I = V/R) in parallel with the same R, or the reverse. The behaviour at the terminals remains identical.

V-I Characteristics of P-N Junction Diode Semiconductor ▾
Core concept

A P-N junction diode allows current in forward bias only. In forward bias (+ve to anode), current rises sharply after the cut-in voltage (~0.7V for Si). In reverse bias, only a tiny leakage current flows until breakdown.

I = I₀(e^(V/ηVT) − 1)  |  VT = 26mV at room temp
Key values to remember

Silicon cut-in voltage ≈ 0.7V. Germanium cut-in voltage ≈ 0.3V. η = 1 for Ge, 2 for Si.

Likely viva questions
Q: How does a diode act as a switch?
A: In forward bias → ON (short circuit, current flows). In reverse bias → OFF (open circuit, current blocked).
Q: What are trivalent and pentavalent impurities?
A: Trivalent (Boron, Aluminium — 3 valence electrons) create holes → P-type semiconductor. Pentavalent (Phosphorus, Arsenic — 5 valence electrons) donate free electrons → N-type semiconductor.
Q: What is dynamic resistance of a diode?
A: r_d = ΔV / ΔI = VT / I. It is the ratio of a small change in voltage to the corresponding change in current at the operating point.

Zener Diode Characteristics Semiconductor ▾
Core concept

A Zener diode is heavily doped and designed to work in reverse breakdown without damage. It maintains a constant voltage (Vz) across it once breakdown occurs — used for voltage regulation.

Two breakdown mechanisms: Zener breakdown (strong electric field rips valence electrons, Vz < 6V) and Avalanche breakdown (carrier multiplication by collision, Vz > 6V).

For 1N4735A used in the experiment: Vz = 6.2V, power = 1W.
Likely viva questions
Q: How is Zener diode different from a normal diode?
A: It is more heavily doped, has a sharp and well-defined breakdown voltage, and is designed to operate continuously in reverse breakdown without damage — unlike a normal diode which gets destroyed.
Q: What is PIV of a Zener diode?
A: Peak Inverse Voltage — the maximum reverse voltage a diode can withstand. For a Zener, it equals the breakdown voltage Vz.
Q: Can a Zener diode be used as a switch?
A: Yes, but it is primarily used as a voltage regulator, not a switch. Its constant voltage in breakdown makes it ideal for reference voltage applications.

Half Wave & Full Wave Rectifiers Power Electronics ▾
Core concept

A rectifier converts AC to pulsating DC. Half wave rectifier: uses 1 diode, only one half of AC passes. Full wave rectifier: uses 2 diodes + centre tap, or 4 diodes (bridge) — both halves are utilised.

Key formulas
Half wave: V_dc = V_m/π ≈ 0.318 V_m  |  Efficiency ≈ 40.6%
Full wave: V_dc = 2V_m/π ≈ 0.636 V_m  |  Efficiency ≈ 81.2%
Ripple factor (r) = V_ac(rms) / V_dc  → smaller r = smoother DC
Likely viva questions
Q: Why is full wave rectifier preferred over half wave?
A: Higher efficiency (81.2% vs 40.6%), lower ripple factor, and better DC output. It utilises both halves of the AC cycle.
Q: What is ripple factor?
A: It is the ratio of the AC component (ripple) to the DC component in the output. Lower ripple = smoother (more pure) DC output.
Q: What does a capacitor filter do?
A: It charges during the peak and discharges during the valley of the pulsating DC, smoothing out the ripples and giving a more steady DC output.

BJT Common Emitter Characteristics Transistor ▾
Core concept

In CE configuration, input is between Base-Emitter and output is between Collector-Emitter. The input characteristics plot I_B vs V_BE (like a diode curve). The output characteristics plot I_C vs V_CE for different fixed I_B values.

β (current gain) = I_C / I_B  |  I_C = β × I_B
Three regions: Active (amplification, I_B controls I_C), Saturation (both junctions forward biased, used in switching ON), Cutoff (transistor OFF, no current).
Likely viva questions
Q: Why is CE configuration preferred for amplification?
A: It gives high current gain (β), high voltage gain, and high power gain compared to CB or CC configurations. It is the most widely used configuration.
Q: What is the range of β for a BJT?
A: Typically 20 to 500, depending on the transistor. For BC107 used in the experiment, β is around 100-300.
Q: Can a transistor be replaced by two back-to-back diodes?
A: No. A transistor has a very thin base that allows minority carrier injection and controlled amplification. Two diodes back-to-back cannot replicate transistor action — the carrier interaction between junctions is absent.

LDR and Photodiode Light Detection Sensors ▾
Core concept

An LDR (Light Dependent Resistor) has very high resistance in darkness and very low resistance in bright light. Light photons excite electrons from valence to conduction band, reducing resistance. Used in automatic street lights, burglar alarms, etc.

R_LDR = R_dark × L^(-b)  → Log R_LDR = Log R_dark − b × Log L
Likely viva questions
Q: How does an LDR work?
A: When light falls on the LDR, photons excite electrons into the conduction band. More electrons = lower resistance = more current. In darkness, resistance is very high (MΩ range).
Q: What is the difference between an LDR and a photodiode?
A: LDR changes its resistance with light intensity (passive, no PN junction). A photodiode is a reverse-biased PN junction that generates photocurrent when light falls on it — faster response, more sensitive, used in high-speed applications.
Q: What is a practical application of an LDR circuit?
A: Automatic street lights (turn on at night, off in day), camera exposure control, burglar alarms, and solar trackers.

Temperature Sensor (LM35) with Arduino Arduino / IoT ▾
Core concept

The LM35 is a precision temperature sensor with output linearly proportional to temperature: 10mV per °C. Connected to Arduino's analog pin, the ADC converts the analog voltage to a digital value for real-time display.

Temperature (°C) = (ADC reading × 5V / 1023) / 0.01V
LM35 pin connections

Pin 1 → +5V (VCC)  |  Pin 2 → Analog pin A0 of Arduino  |  Pin 3 → GND

Likely viva questions
Q: What is the sensitivity of LM35?
A: 10mV per degree Celsius. So at 25°C it gives 250mV output.
Q: What is an RTD?
A: Resistance Temperature Detector — a sensor whose resistance changes predictably with temperature. More accurate than thermocouples over moderate temperature ranges.
Q: What does ADC mean in Arduino?
A: Analog to Digital Converter. Arduino has a 10-bit ADC, so it gives values from 0 to 1023 corresponding to 0V to 5V input. Used to read analog sensor outputs.

Op-Amp configurations (IC741) Beyond syllabus ▾
Three configurations
Inverting amplifier
Input at (−) terminal. Output is inverted. Gain A_v = −R_F / R₁ (negative = 180° phase shift).
Non-inverting amplifier
Input at (+) terminal. Output is in phase. Gain A_v = 1 + R_F / R₁ (always ≥ 1).
Voltage follower (buffer)
Output connected directly to (−) input. Gain = 1 (unity). Very high input impedance, very low output impedance — used for impedance matching.
Key viva question
Q: What is virtual ground?
A: In an inverting amplifier, the (−) input is at 0V (same as ground) even though it is not physically connected to ground. This happens due to the very high open-loop gain and negative feedback, which forces V₊ ≈ V₋ ≈ 0V.
